08 — Methodology

Indicators, Sources, & Assumptions

This page documents every analytical choice made in constructing this site: which indicators are tracked, where data comes from, what the known limitations are, and what assumptions the framework rests on. It is modeled after academic supplementary materials.

The function of this page is to distinguish analysis from advocacy. Any reader should be able to disagree with our methodology, identify our assumptions, and check our sources. If the framework is sound, it should survive scrutiny. If it has flaws, this is where they should be findable.

Section 1 — Analytical framework

The site's core framework divides authoritarian development into three phases: Preconditions (structural vulnerabilities that enable authoritarian emergence), Emergence (the active phase where specific mechanisms are deployed), and Consolidation (structural changes that entrench power and make reversal harder). This framework is derived from the comparative politics literature, primarily:

Levitsky & Ziblatt (2018), "How Democracies Die" — provides the core framework for identifying warning signs within democratic systems, distinguishing democratic backsliding from revolutionary authoritarianism. Their "litmus tests" for authoritarian behavior (rejection of democratic rules, denial of legitimacy to opponents, toleration of violence, willingness to curtail civil liberties) inform the event classification on this site.

Linz (1978), "The Breakdown of Democratic Regimes" — foundational typology of democratic breakdown mechanisms; distinguishes between authoritarian, totalitarian, and post-totalitarian regimes. Informs the comparator selection (Hungary, Turkey, Venezuela are "competitive authoritarian" regimes; Cuba and China are not).

Levitsky & Way (2010), "Competitive Authoritarianism" — defines the hybrid regime type most relevant to contemporary backsliding cases: formal democratic institutions combined with systematic incumbent advantage. The Hungarian and Turkish profiles in the comparators section draw directly on this typology.

The three-phase framework is not predictive of outcome. It is a classification tool that allows consistent coding of events across time and comparison across cases. Events can be assigned to phases based on their mechanism type (precondition indicator, emergence mechanism, consolidation mechanism) — not based on an overall assessment of "how authoritarian" the current situation is. That overall assessment is not made explicitly on this site.

Section 2 — Primary data sources

V-Dem (Varieties of Democracy)
University of Gothenburg — V-Dem Institute
Democracy indices & indicators
The most comprehensive available dataset of democracy indicators, covering 202 countries from 1789 to the present. Includes 500+ indicators across electoral, liberal, participatory, deliberative, and egalitarian democracy dimensions. Country-year estimates are derived from expert coding supplemented by quantitative data. The Liberal Democracy Index (LDI) used in historical comparator profiles is the v2x_libdem variable in V-Dem Dataset v13 (2023).
Limitation: Expert-coded indicators introduce inter-rater variability; coders are drawn disproportionately from academic networks. Real-time updates are not available — data typically lags 12–18 months. V-Dem data for the United States 2025–2026 is not yet available; this site does not compute a composite DHI score pending that data.
Freedom House
Freedom House (Washington DC)
Annual country assessments
Annual "Freedom in the World" and "Freedom of the Press" assessments covering political rights, civil liberties, and press freedom for 195 countries. Assessments are based on a structured scoring rubric applied by regional analysts. Used in this site primarily for historical comparator status assessments (Hungary, Turkey, Venezuela classifications).
Limitation: Freedom House receives substantial US government funding (NED, USAID), which creates a perception of bias — the organization is generally seen as more critical of left-wing authoritarian governments than right-wing ones. This site cross-checks Freedom House assessments against V-Dem and Polity5 where available.
Polity5
Center for Systemic Peace
Regime authority scores
Long-running dataset assigning each country-year a Polity score from −10 (full autocracy) to +10 (full democracy) based on the competitiveness of political participation, executive recruitment, and executive constraints. Used on this site primarily for historical longitudinal context, not for current US assessment.
Limitation: The Polity framework focuses heavily on formal institutional attributes and is less sensitive to informal norm erosion — exactly the type of backsliding most relevant to Hungary, Turkey, and the current US context. A country can score +9 on Polity while undergoing significant informal democratic erosion.
Primary news sources
Multiple outlets — see individual event citations
Event documentation
Individual events on this site are sourced to primary news sources, official documents (executive orders, court filings, congressional testimony, agency press releases), and legal databases (Westlaw, CourtListener) where applicable. Events are not included based on a single source; where possible, multiple independent sources confirm.
Limitation: All events in the current version are hardcoded with illustrative sourcing. In v2, events will link to verified primary sources. Users should independently verify any specific event before relying on it for research or reporting purposes.
Protect Democracy / Trump Action Tracker
Protect Democracy (protectdemocracy.org)
Event log reference
Protect Democracy's authoritarian action watch and the independent Trump Action Tracker (trumpactiontracker.info) are used as reference logs for event identification. These organizations maintain rolling inventories of democratic norm violations with sourcing. This site independently classifies events using its own phase framework rather than adopting their classification schemes.
Limitation: Both organizations are explicitly advocacy-oriented in addition to tracking. This site uses them for event identification only, applying its own analytical framework independently.

Section 3 — Indicator definitions

Phase classification
Event-level indicator — all pages
Each event is classified as Phase 1, 2, or 3 based on its mechanism type, not on an overall assessment of where the US currently stands. Phase 1 events are indicators of structural preconditions (suppression of economic data, institutional trust metrics). Phase 2 events are active mechanism deployments (executive orders targeting specific groups, surveillance expansions, propaganda patterns). Phase 3 events are structural changes that would be difficult to reverse (constitutional reinterpretation, court packing, biometric infrastructure that persists beyond any administration).
Derived from: Levitsky & Ziblatt (2018); Bermeo (2016) "On Democratic Backsliding"
Known limitation: Phase boundaries are not always clean. An executive order may have both Phase 2 (deployment) and Phase 3 (structural) characteristics. Where ambiguous, events are classified by their most significant long-term mechanism.
Institutional severity score
Event-level indicator — index page
Five-point dot scale reflecting the estimated difficulty of reversing the event's institutional effects. Score 1: no structural change, purely rhetorical. Score 2: administrative change, easily reversed by subsequent executive action. Score 3: structural change, requires deliberate reversal action but achievable within normal democratic processes. Score 4: changes to institutions whose reversal requires legislative supermajority or extended political effort. Score 5: constitutional or de facto permanent changes.
Scoring is editorial judgment by site authors, not derived from a specific index.
Known limitation: Severity scores are the most subjective element on this site. Two reasonable people may score the same event differently. In v2, severity scores will be assessed by a panel rather than a single rater.
Mechanism taxonomy
Classification system — propaganda & rights page
Events on the propaganda and rights tracker are classified into three mechanism categories: Propaganda (narrative control, scapegoating, enemy framing, manufactured consent), Coercive (censorship by intimidation, detention, surveillance-enabled enforcement), and Legal/Institutional (use of existing legal mechanisms — licensing, contracting, regulatory review — to achieve censorship or suppression outcomes without explicit constitutional challenge). The taxonomy is derived from Hannah Arendt's "Origins of Totalitarianism" (mechanism categories), Carl Schmitt's state of exception literature (legal mechanism analysis), and IB History DP authoritarian states curriculum.
Primary: Arendt (1951); Schmitt (1922); Paxton (2004) "Anatomy of Fascism"
Known limitation: The taxonomy is simplified for accessibility. Academic literature on authoritarian communication distinguishes many more subcategories. Events may fit multiple categories; the primary mechanism is used for classification.
Scientific institution integrity status
Institution-level indicator — environment page
Three-tier status system for federal scientific institutions: Green (no significant changes reported in staffing, funding, or publications), Yellow (significant changes in one dimension), Red (significant changes in two or more dimensions, or one severe structural change such as an entire office elimination or mass Schedule F reclassification). Status is based on documented actions, not on assessment of scientific output quality.
Derived from: environmental and scientific watchdog reporting; official agency announcements; academic freedom monitoring organizations
Known limitation: "No significant changes reported" (green) is not the same as "no significant changes occurred." This system tracks documented changes; undocumented or unreported changes cannot be captured.
Resilience court ruling significance
Event-level indicator — hope & resilience page
Court rulings are assessed on two dimensions: (1) immediate operational effect — does the ruling constrain an administration action currently in force, or is it procedural? (2) durability — is the ruling likely to be upheld on appeal given circuit and Supreme Court composition? Significance notes explicitly address both dimensions.
Assessment is based on legal analysis from public interest law organizations and academic legal commentary, not this site's own legal expertise.
Known limitation: Predicting appellate outcomes involves genuine uncertainty. Significance assessments reflect the state of legal analysis at the time of the ruling, not ultimate outcomes.

Section 4 — Site assumptions (explicit)

Assumption 01
Comparative historical analysis is the appropriate method for assessing current democratic health
This site assumes that events have meaning beyond their immediate context — that an executive order targeting judges is better understood in light of Hungary 2011 than in isolation, and that this comparative framing reveals something real about mechanism type and trajectory. The alternative view is that all political events are context-specific and historical comparison is misleading.
Challenge: The US institutional context (federal system, First Amendment, independent military, strong civil society) differs significantly from Hungary in 2010. Mechanisms that succeeded there may not succeed here. This site does not claim they will; it claims the mechanism type is recognizable.
Assumption 02
The framework applies symmetrically across the political spectrum
The three-phase framework is designed to identify authoritarian consolidation regardless of ideological direction. The comparator set includes left-wing (Cuba, Venezuela/Chávez) and right-wing (Hungary, Turkey) cases. The indicators are defined in terms of mechanism type — media control, judicial manipulation, surveillance — not political content.
Challenge: Selecting which events to track involves editorial judgment, and the current US events tracked on this site reflect events occurring under a right-wing administration. A future version tracking a left-wing administration using similar mechanisms should apply the same framework. The methodology does not change with the political direction of the actor.
Assumption 03
Democratic norms have intrinsic value beyond their procedural function
This site treats democratic institutions — judicial independence, press freedom, free and fair elections — as valuable in themselves, not merely as means to good policy outcomes. An authoritarian government may produce some good policy outcomes; this site's framework would still identify its mechanisms as authoritarian. This reflects a substantive commitment to liberal democracy as a value, not merely an analytical stance.
Challenge: This is a normative assumption, not a factual one. Readers who believe democratic procedures are only instrumentally valuable — worth preserving only when they produce good outcomes — will disagree with the site's framing. This disagreement is legitimate and should be acknowledged.
Assumption 04
Resilience indicators are analytically as important as authoritarian indicators
The hope and resilience page is not included as a disclaimer or to soften the site's analysis — it reflects the actual historical record. In every case of democratic backsliding, significant institutional and civil society resistance occurred. The resistance sometimes succeeded (Chile, South Korea, Poland); it sometimes failed (Hungary, Turkey, Venezuela). Accurately representing both dimensions is required for methodological integrity.
Challenge: There is a selection effect in resilience assessment: the cases where resistance "worked" may be structurally different from current US conditions in ways that make comparison misleading. This limitation is noted in the hope & resilience page's historical parallels section.
Assumption 05
Transparency about limitations does not undermine the analysis
This site's approach is that explicit acknowledgment of methodological limitations — in this methodology page and in individual indicator definitions — strengthens rather than weakens the analysis. A site that claims certainty it does not have would be less credible than one that maps its uncertainty accurately.
Challenge: Extensive caveating can be used rhetorically to create false equivalence — "both sides have limitations, therefore nothing can be concluded." This site's caveats are specific and bounded; they do not imply that all conclusions are equally uncertain.

Section 5 — Event tagging ontology

Events across the site are tagged using a structured vocabulary. The primary dimensions are mechanism type, affected group, phase, and policy type. The full vocabulary is defined below.

Tag Dimension Definition
Propaganda Mechanism type Narrative control mechanisms: scapegoating, enemy framing, manufactured consent, information environment manipulation
Coercive Mechanism type Force or intimidation mechanisms: detention, censorship by intimidation, surveillance-enabled enforcement, paramilitary deployment
Legal / Institutional Mechanism type Use of existing legal mechanisms to achieve authoritarian outcomes: licensing, contracting, regulatory review, reclassification
Executive order Policy type Direct presidential orders invoking executive authority; does not require congressional approval
Agency restructuring Policy type Organizational changes to federal agencies: eliminating offices, replacing leadership, merging functions
Regulatory rollback Policy type Suspension or reversal of existing rules and enforcement standards
Intl. withdrawal Policy type Withdrawal from international agreements or bodies that provided external accountability
Women Affected group Events disproportionately targeting women's rights, healthcare, political representation, or bodily autonomy
LGBTQ+ Affected group Events targeting LGBTQ+ rights, identity recognition, healthcare access, or legal status
Press Affected group Events targeting journalists, news organizations, or the broader information ecosystem
Immigrants Affected group Events targeting non-citizens, asylum seekers, undocumented persons, or legal immigrants
Political opposition Affected group Events targeting elected officials, political parties, civil society organizations, or political activists
Phase 1 Phase Structural preconditions: economic data suppression, institutional trust metrics, polarization indicators
Phase 2 Phase Active mechanism deployment: specific executive actions, propaganda patterns, surveillance expansions
Phase 3 Phase Consolidation: structural changes difficult to reverse through normal democratic processes

Section 6 — What this site does not claim

This site does not claim that the United States is currently an authoritarian state. The framework tracks mechanisms and indicators, not conclusions. Individual event classifications are based on mechanism type. An overall assessment of democratic health requires a composite index, which this site has deferred pending methodological decisions about V-Dem indicator weighting and real-time data availability.

This site does not claim the US trajectory is irreversible. Every historical comparator case included in the site has experienced some form of resistance. In cases where resistance succeeded (Chile, South Korea, Poland, Spain), it succeeded in part because of factors this site tracks on the hope and resilience page. The site documents both authoritarian mechanisms and countervailing forces with equal seriousness.

This site does not claim the framework predicts outcome. The three-phase framework is a diagnostic and classification tool. It can identify mechanism types and track their deployment; it cannot predict how far consolidation will proceed, whether it will succeed, or what countervailing forces will prevent it. Prediction would require a probabilistic model this site does not attempt.

This site does not claim its event selection is complete. The events tracked here represent a curated, illustrative set. Many qualifying events are not included. The framework does not claim to be a comprehensive database; it claims to be an analytically consistent selection from a larger universe of events.

This site does not claim neutrality. The framework assumes that democratic institutions — judicial independence, press freedom, rule of law, separation of powers — have value. Tracking their erosion is not neutral; it reflects a commitment to those values. What the site claims is methodological consistency: the same framework would track left-wing authoritarian mechanisms with equal rigor. "Analysis, not advocacy" means the analytical framework is not deployed in service of a partisan position — not that the site has no values.

Section 7 — Open methodological questions

Open question 01
Y-axis composition for the main DHI chart
The planned main visualization requires a composite Democratic Health Index as the y-axis. This requires decisions about: which V-Dem sub-indicators to include; how to weight them; how to handle disagreements between V-Dem, Freedom House, and Polity5 on the same country-year; and whether to use V-Dem's pre-built liberal democracy index or construct a custom composite. This decision has been deferred. Using a pre-built index (V-Dem LDI) is simpler and more transparent; constructing a custom index allows more precise targeting but introduces additional methodological choices that require justification.
Open question 02
NLP sentiment analysis methodology
The v2 sentiment analysis feature (placeholders currently on propaganda, environment, and militarized tech pages) requires decisions about: which platforms to include; how to handle bot activity; how to define demographic segments; how to handle platform API access changes; what baseline period to use; and how to address the well-documented problem that social media data overrepresents certain demographics. These questions are unresolved.
Open question 03
Update cadence and maintenance
The site currently contains hardcoded data through May 2026. For ongoing utility, events need to be added regularly. This requires decisions about: how frequently to update; who reviews events for inclusion; what quality standards apply; and whether events should be added individually or in batches. No update process has been established for the current version.
