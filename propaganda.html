02 — Propaganda & Rights Tracker

Rhetoric, Narrative Control, & Minority Targeting

This page tracks the specific mechanisms political scientists and historians use to identify authoritarian communication patterns and the systematic erosion of minority rights — including scapegoating, manufactured consent, legal censorship, and enemy framing. Each mechanism is defined against documented historical cases and mapped to observable current events.

Minority groups are disproportionately targeted during authoritarian consolidation because they serve a structural function: concentrating public fear or resentment onto a defined outgroup legitimizes emergency authority, weakens coalition-building across civil society, and allows rights-erosion to begin in populations with limited political power before spreading to the general public.

All events on this page are sourced. Historical comparisons are labeled as such and are analytical, not rhetorical — the same framework applies to any direction of democratic backsliding.

Section 2 — Mechanism taxonomy

Propaganda Coercive Legal / Institutional
Propaganda mechanism
Scapegoating

The systematic attribution of societal problems — economic anxiety, crime, cultural decline — to a specific outgroup in order to consolidate majority support and justify exceptional measures against that group.

Historical parallel — Hungary (Orbán, 2015–present): State-funded "national consultation" campaigns blamed migrants and financier George Soros for Hungary's economic and security problems, laying groundwork for border wall construction and refugee detention.

Immigrants Ethnic minorities
Propaganda mechanism
Manufactured consent

The deliberate shaping of public information — through state media control, selective omission, or narrative framing — to create the appearance of popular support for policies that serve consolidating power.

Historical parallel — China (Mao, 1949–1976): The People's Daily and Xinhua news agency operated as exclusive state information channels, systematically omitting famine data, purge casualties, and Cultural Revolution violence from public record.
Historically targeted groups
Press Political opposition
Propaganda mechanism
Enemy framing

The rhetorical construction of a domestic or foreign enemy whose opposition to the regime is presented as evidence of existential threat, used to delegitimize political opponents and justify emergency action.

Historical parallel — Turkey (Erdoğan, 2013–present): Kurdish political leaders, secular journalists, and opposition academics were systematically labeled "terrorists" in official statements and prosecuted under anti-terrorism statutes, regardless of involvement in violence.
Historically targeted groups
Political opposition Ethnic/religious minorities
Coercive mechanism
Censorship by intimidation

The suppression of press or civil society through implicit or explicit threats — financial pressure, license revocation, personal targeting of journalists — without requiring formal legal censorship.

Historical parallel — Venezuela (Chávez, 2002–2010): State regulator CONATEL revoked broadcast licenses for critical television stations following the 2002 coup attempt. Remaining private outlets shifted coverage after watching competitors shut down, without direct censorship orders.
Historically targeted groups
Press Civil society
Legal / Institutional mechanism
Legal censorship

The use of existing legal mechanisms — licensing, regulatory enforcement, sedition laws, or procedural tools — to restrict press and civil society activity through ostensibly legitimate channels.

Historical parallel — Cuba (Castro, 1960): Law 1180 nationalized all private media outlets, formally eliminating independent press through a legal property instrument rather than an explicit censorship decree, making reversal structurally difficult.
Historically targeted groups
Press Civil society
